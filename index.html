About Me

I approach research through first‑principles analysis of energy, chemical, and industrial systems. I connect fundamental mechanisms with applied technological development, emphasizing efficiency and scalability. I examine how technology interacts with economic and policy structures and treat engineering and institutional design as complementary elements of system performance. My focus is disciplined execution, clear strategies, and converting rigorous analysis into measurable progress.

Research & Academic Work

My academic training is in Metallurgical Engineering, with experimental M.Sc. and Ph.D. work centered on reaction kinetics, hydrometallurgical systems, and mineral–solution interactions. My doctoral research focused on the cyanidation kinetics of silver telluride, combining hydrometallurgical and electrochemical experimentation with kinetic and thermodynamic modeling and advanced mineral characterization.

My work has covered mineral dissolution mechanisms, hydrometallurgical extraction processes, kinetic and thermodynamic analysis, mineral characterization, and technical evaluation of mining operations, including reserve assessment and resource exploitation. I have supervised research projects in these areas and lead the academic body Tecnología y Ciencia de los Recursos Minerales.

This technical background supports my current transition toward energy-related systems, where reaction kinetics, high-temperature behavior, and process modeling remain core analytical tools.
