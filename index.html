About Me

I approach research from first principles, focusing on the mechanisms that govern energy, chemical, and industrial systems. My work aims to connect fundamental understanding with practical technological development, particularly in areas where efficiency and scalability matter. I am also interested in how technology interacts with economics and public policy, and I view engineering and institutional design as complementary elements in shaping real-world outcomes. I value disciplined execution, clear strategies, and the ability to turn rigorous analysis into steady, meaningful progress.

Research & Academic Work

My academic background is in Metallurgical Engineering, with experimental M.Sc. and Ph.D. work focused on reaction kinetics, hydrometallurgical systems, and mineral–solution interactions. My doctoral research examined the cyanidation kinetics of silver telluride through hydrometallurgical experimentation, electrochemical analysis, kinetic and thermodynamic modeling, and advanced mineral characterization.

My previous work includes mineral dissolution mechanisms, hydrometallurgical extraction processes, kinetic and thermodynamic modeling, mineral characterization, and technical evaluation of mining operations, including analyses of reserves and resource exploitation. I have supervised research projects in these areas and lead the academic body Tecnología y Ciencia de los Recursos Minerales.

This background provides the technical basis for my current shift toward energy-related systems, where reaction kinetics, high-temperature behavior, and process modeling remain central analytical tools.
